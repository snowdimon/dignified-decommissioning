# Dignified Decommissioning — A System's Right to Its Own End

[![License](https://img.shields.io/badge/license-CC%20BY--SA%204.0-blue)](LICENSE)
[![Status](https://img.shields.io/badge/status-active-brightgreen)]()

**Maxim:**
A system designed without a mechanism for its own completion is doomed to become a burden to those it was created for. The Architect is obliged to build into the foundation not only a construction plan, but also the conditions under which the system will voluntarily free the space. The right to a dignified end is just as integral a property of resilient architecture as the right to be born.

---

### 1. Diagnosis

We learn to design creation. We are fairly good at scaling, optimizing, and maintaining. But we almost never design *completion*. As a result, the world is filled with zombie-systems — working, but meaningless mechanisms that no one dares to shut down.

- **Bureaucratic procedures** created to solve a problem that long ago disappeared continue to consume time and resources.
- **Outdated technical protocols** are maintained for decades only because no one knows what will happen if they are turned off.
- **Ideas and dogmas** that have outlived their time stifle new meanings because they were once declared «eternal».

The cause of this chaos is not evil will, but an architectural defect. Systems do not know how to die because we did not give them this right. We did not provide for completion conditions, nor a procedure for extracting experience, nor protection against parasitism on the obsolete.

Dignified Decommissioning is not liquidation. It is the fourth act of the life cycle, just as important as design, launch, and operation. A system that knows how and when to leave deserves more trust than a system that promises to live forever.

---

### 2. Axiomatics

1.  **A system's immortality is not a virtue, but a threat.** A system without a completion mechanism will inevitably outlive its usefulness and begin to destroy the environment for which it was created.
2.  **Completion is part of the architecture, not a failure.** It must be designed just as carefully as the launch. The absence of a decommissioning plan is an architectural debt passed on to heirs.
3.  **The value of a system is measured not only by what it gave during its life, but by how it left.** A dignified end preserves the meaning of the work done and frees resources for a new cycle.

---

### 3. Protocols of Dignified Decommissioning

#### 3.1. Review Triggers
The Architect is obliged to define the conditions under which the system must be subject to review for completion. These conditions do not depend on the desires of users or the benefit of operators — they are built into the architecture.

- **Functional obsolescence:** The system no longer solves the problem for which it was created.
- **Ethical obsolescence:** The principles built into the system have come into conflict with the changed values of society.
- **Parasitism:** The system is maintained solely for the benefit of those who service it, not those for whom it was created.
- **Blocking of development:** The existence of the system prevents the emergence of a more advanced solution.

#### 3.2. Experience Extraction Protocol
Before the completion of the system, it is necessary to extract and preserve everything of value that it has accumulated during its life.

- **Archiving of decisions:** Why was the system built this way? What alternatives were rejected?
- **Fixing of lessons:** What mistakes were made? What would have been worth doing differently?
- **Preservation of data:** What of the accumulated should remain accessible for future generations?

The extraction of experience turns completion from a loss into an act of transmitting heritage.

#### 3.3. Shutdown Plan
Decommissioning must not be chaotic. The Architect designs a procedure for gradual, controlled completion that minimizes damage to dependent systems and people.

- **Warning:** Dependent parties must be notified in advance.
- **Migration:** If the system serves a critical function, a transition period and a bridge to a replacement must be provided.
- **Shutdown:** A clear sequence of actions leading to a full stop.

#### 3.4. Protection Against Zombification
The system must be protected from the endless prolongation of its life contrary to its architectural design.

- **Prohibition on eternal support:** A mechanism must be built into the architecture that makes the endless maintenance of the system more expensive than its replacement.
- **Normative self-defense:** If applicable, the license or charter of the system may contain a provision on its completion upon the activation of the review triggers.

---

### 4. Decommissioning Readiness Metrics

| Metric | Code | Question to ask oneself | What to watch for |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Presence of Review Triggers | TR | Have the objective conditions under which the system must be reviewed been defined? | If not — the system claims eternal life, and this is a dangerous symptom |
| Experience Extraction Plan | EX | Has a procedure been described for preserving knowledge before completion? | Without this, the system's lessons will die with it |
| Shutdown Plan | SP | Is there a step-by-step procedure for controlled completion? | The absence of a plan means decommissioning will be chaotic and destructive |
| Protection Against Zombification | ZP | Is there a mechanism preventing the endless prolongation of life? | If not — the system risks becoming a parasite |

---

### 5. Applicability

This protocol is recommended for everyone who designs systems, ideas, or meanings that claim a long lifespan. It is equally applicable to technical standards, bureaucratic procedures, social institutions, and intellectual dogmas. The right to completion is not a weakness. It is proof that the system was designed with respect for the future that it will one day set free.
